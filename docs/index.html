Regenerative Potential Index – Identification and Assessment in Amsterdam

2024, PhD Research

The Regenerative Potential Index (RPI) is an evaluation framework for assessing the regenerative potential of urban areas — applicable to both public and private sectors. Rather than measuring sustainability in isolation, the RPI asks a forward-looking question: how capable is a territory of regenerating itself? It aggregates multiple indicators across four dimensions — Economic, Environmental, Political and Social — into a single composite score (0–100) per territorial unit per year.

This case study applies the RPI to the eight districts of the municipality of Amsterdam over a decade of data (2015–2024). The municipality's overall RPI in 2024 stands at 48.42, down from 50.19 in 2015. This modest decline conceals important contrasts: the Environmental dimension improved (+1.46 pts) while Economic performance deteriorated sharply (–7.21 pts, reaching 43.01 in 2024 — the most constrained dimension). The COVID-19 pandemic left a visible shock in 2020–2021, particularly on Economic and Political indicators.

RPI by Dimension – Time Evolution

Not all dimensions evolve the same way. The Environmental dimension is the strongest (51.53 in 2024) and has been improving, driven by air quality and energy transitions. The Economic dimension is the weakest (43.01) and has declined the most since 2015, reflecting structural shifts in the local economy. District Comparison – 2024 Snapshot

Which districts perform best — and worst — in 2024? The bar chart ranks all eight districts plus the municipality aggregate (purple) for each dimension. Significant spatial inequality exists within Amsterdam: some districts score well above average on Social indicators while lagging on Economic ones. Scenario Projections – 2030 & 2050

Where is Amsterdam heading? Three scenarios are projected from 2024 onwards using percentile-based annual change rates derived from the historical record: Constrained (pessimistic, p25 trend) forecasts a decline to 35.79 by 2050; Business-As-Usual (BAU, p50 trend) projects a marginal improvement to 49.34; Optimistic (p75 trend) reaches 57.44. The gap between scenarios highlights how sensitive regenerative trajectories are to policy choices made in the next few years.
